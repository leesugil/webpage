Perlin Noise

Perlin noise is a noise-generating algorithm invented by Ken Perlin in 1983 and has been one of the most popular algorithms to generate a noise that is "smoothly random."

It is particularly useful in computer graphics, especially in terrain-generation, because, unlike a pure random sequence representing unrealistic up and down of the height of a terrain, a random sequence generated by Perlin noise shows that the close numbers have similar heights, though they transit smoothly so that we still gain a long-term random fluctuation.

There are many articles on the Internet that explains the algorithm as it's been incredibly popular (and it is not-so-challenging to comprehend), so I'll skil the explanation this time until I feel necessary in the future. Basically it uses a vector field on a lattice (called gradient vectors) to determine the general tendancy of the pattern, whereas the gradation effect (the "smooth" transition of brightness) is obtained by a choice of an interpolation function.

Compare this to a 2-dimensional array made out of purely random brightness.

This one requires a random number per each pixel, which is a lot more than the number of random vectors we needed on a lattice in Perlin noise. That's why it looks a lot more chaotic, whereas a Perlin noise has a smooth transition between sparse random values.
